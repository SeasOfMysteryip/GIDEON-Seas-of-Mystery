### EP203_A01S04 — Prying Eyes

**INT. THE SALTY SIREN — NASSAU — NIGHT**

A ceiling fan turns slowly through cigarette haze and sea heat.

The Salty Siren is not trying to be beautiful. Warped wood. Salt-blurred windows. Beer signs with dead bulbs. A radio behind the bar fighting with a tourist laughing too loud near the door.

A server drops two sweating bottles onto a table. Glass knocks wood. Foam crawls down a brown neck.

At the bar, a WOMAN’S HAND rests beside an untouched rum and coke.

That is all we get of her.

No face. No name. No uniform. No mark.

Just the hand. Clean nails. Still fingers. A thumb that does not drum, does not fidget, does not drink.

In her ear, tucked mostly under loose hair, a tiny flesh-colored earpiece catches the room.

The bar noise dulls.

A voice comes through, filtered and close.

**CARLOS (V.O., THROUGH EARPIECE)**  
I’m telling you, it wasn’t the same line. Not the same water, not the same drift, not the same damn sky.

A second voice answers, younger, amused because he wants not to be scared.

**FINNIAN (V.O., THROUGH EARPIECE)**  
The sky was still above us, Carlos. That’s where it usually keeps itself.

A few laughs crackle through the feed.

The woman’s hand slides half an inch toward the glass. Stops.

Across the room, tucked into a corner booth under a mounted marlin with one cloudy eye, CARLOS, MARTIN, FINNIAN, CALLUM, TOBY, and BRAM sit around a table crowded with beers, baskets of fries, wet napkins, and the messy relief of people who have not yet admitted they were afraid.

They are not briefing.

They are coming down.

Bram has both elbows on the table and a bottle held against his forehead. Toby peels the label off his beer in one long nervous strip. Callum keeps glancing toward the front windows by habit. Martin looks like he has been trying to relax for ten minutes and failing.

Carlos has the table because Carlos cannot let the pattern go.

**CARLOS**  
Laugh all you want. The compass didn’t wander. It pulled.

**MARTIN**  
Compasses don’t pull.

**CARLOS**  
This one did.

**TOBY**  
Maybe it just fancied you.

**FINNIAN**  
That would explain the spinning.

Another little burst of laughter. Too loud for one second, then it dies because they all remember the deck under their feet.

At the bar, the woman’s hand remains still.

The earpiece catches everything poorly and well at once: ice in glasses, a chair scrape, Martin’s low voice, Carlos leaning in.

**CARLOS**  
Listen to me. We’ve been near the Triangle before. Skirted it. Watched the instruments get stupid. Watched Gus pretend they weren’t getting stupid.

**CALLUM**  
Gus hears you say that, he’ll make you clean the bilge with your own shirt.

**CARLOS**  
Gus knows exactly what I’m saying. That’s why he turned us off it.

That lands differently.

The table settles.

**BRAM**  
We weren’t going in.

**CARLOS**  
No. We weren’t. That’s the point.

He takes a drink. Not dramatic. Just buying himself one second.

**CARLOS**  
We weren’t trying to go in, and we still got closer than we’ve ever managed.

At the bar, the woman’s thumb presses once against the side of the glass.

**MARTIN**  
Closer to what?

Carlos gives him a look.

Martin knows. They all know. He asked anyway because the word is worse when said out loud.

**CARLOS**  
The Fracture Zone.

The radio behind the bar hits static for half a breath, then finds the song again.

Nobody at the booth notices.

The woman at the bar does.

**FINNIAN**  
You make it sound like a dock.

**CARLOS**  
I wish it was a dock. A dock has posts. Numbers. Rotting rope. Something you can measure without wanting to throw the chart overboard.

**TOBY**  
So what did we measure?

Carlos pulls a damp napkin from under a basket and smooths it on the table. He does not draw a map. He knows better than that in public.

He uses bottle caps instead.

One cap for the Gull. One for Nassau. One for nothing at all.

**CARLOS**  
Here’s where we should’ve been.

He taps the first cap.

**CARLOS**  
Here’s where the radar thought we were.

Second cap.

**CARLOS**  
Here’s where the light broke.

The third cap.

**BRAM**  
Light doesn’t break.

**CALLUM**  
It did.

Callum says it quietly enough that the joke dies before it can be born.

Carlos nods, grateful for the backup.

**CARLOS**  
Two frames. That’s all Linda got. Two frames where the sky gave us aircraft that weren’t there before and weren’t there after.

**MARTIN**  
You’re talking about the five planes.

**FINNIAN**  
The ghost planes.

**CARLOS**  
I’m talking about five aircraft moving through broken light, because if I say ghost planes in a bar in Nassau, I deserve whatever happens next.

A couple of them laugh again, smaller this time.

The woman’s hand moves from the glass to a folded cocktail napkin beside it.

Not writing yet.

Listening.

**TOBY**  
But we’re back. That matters, doesn’t it?

**BRAM**  
Back with passengers thinking they got a very authentic pirate cruise and a free near-death experience.

**FINNIAN**  
They tipped badly for people who survived the impossible.

**MARTIN**  
They don’t know they survived the impossible.

**CALLUM**  
That was the work.

That line has weight because it is true.

The cover worked. The passengers disembarked. The Gull came in under daylight and noise and tourist complaints, not panic.

For one beat, the table lets itself have that.

**BRAM**  
To the work, then.

He lifts his beer.

The others do too, some willing, some tired, some only because not lifting it would mean saying what they are really thinking.

Bottles touch.

**ALL / OVERLAPPING**  
To the work. / Aye. / To not sinking. / To Gus not murdering us.

They drink.

At the bar, the woman does not.

**MARTIN**  
Carlos.

Carlos looks over.

**MARTIN**  
If we got closer without trying, what happens when somebody tries?

The table quiets again.

That is the question nobody wanted to bring to beer.

Carlos does not have a good answer. That bothers him more than the question.

**CARLOS**  
Then they better know what they’re following.

**FINNIAN**  
And if they don’t?

Carlos looks down at the bottle caps.

The cap standing in for nothing-at-all gleams in a puddle of beer.

**CARLOS**  
Then the water teaches them.

A bar laugh erupts from another table. Someone drops a dart. A woman near the jukebox swears at a man who absolutely deserves it.

The Salty Siren rolls on, warm and indifferent.

At the bar, the observer finally moves.

She takes the cocktail napkin.

With a short pencil no one saw her holding, she writes only four words.

**CLOSER THAN BEFORE.**

She pauses.

Then adds one more.

**GULL.**

She folds the napkin once. Twice.

The earpiece catches Carlos again, softer now, half-buried under the return of ordinary conversation.

**CARLOS (V.O., THROUGH EARPIECE)**  
Anyway. Drink your beer. We earned one quiet hour before the world gets clever again.

The woman’s hand closes around the folded napkin.

She leaves the rum and coke untouched.

A few bills appear under the glass.

No rush. No chase. No glance back.

Only the empty stool, the wet ring on the bar, and the crew still laughing in the corner because for one hour they believed the danger had stayed outside.

The front door opens.

Nassau night breathes in.

The observer exits without a face.

The door shuts softly behind her.

**CUT TO BLACK.**

## EP203 A1S4 — Prying Eyes Beat Outline
## Scene Purpose

Turn a casual Nassau decompression scene into a quiet exposure beat. The crew believes they are talking among themselves after surviving the Gull’s latest return, but the audience experiences the conversation through someone else’s access.

## Entry State

The Bloody Gull has returned to dock. The crew has survived the A01S01 journey and is trying to breathe again in an ordinary Nassau space.

## Exit State

Their public concealment may have held, but their private pattern has leaked. Someone nearby is close enough to hear, smart enough to understand, and patient enough to keep watching.

## Beat Spine

1. **Open inside the Salty Siren.**
   - Keep it small: bar noise, Nassau texture, beer, decompression.
   - Do not stage it like an intelligence room or surveillance operation.

2. **Establish the observer before the crew table.**
   - We see only a non-identifying part of her.
   - No face, no name, no faction marker, no clear uniform, no reveal.
   - The audience understands there is a body in the room, but not who she is.

3. **The crew conversation arrives through her earpiece.**
   - Carlos, Martin, Finnian, Callum, Toby, and Bram are heard before they are treated as a normal table scene.
   - The sound should feel filtered through the observer’s listening position.
   - This makes the table talk feel exposed from the start.

4. **The table is decompressing, not briefing.**
   - They have beers.
   - They are coming down from the Gull’s return and the A01S01 journey.
   - The scene should feel like crew talk that accidentally contains useful information.

5. **Carlos explains the key pattern.**
   - Carlos lays out what happened and why.
   - The essential point: the Gull got closer to the Bermuda Triangle Fracture Zone than they have managed so far.
   - Keep this practical, not myth-heavy.

6. **The observer separates noise from signal.**
   - She does not interrupt, confront, or reveal herself.
   - She gathers the useful detail by listening.
   - The scene pressure is comprehension: she understands enough for the talk to matter.

7. **The crew’s concealment problem turns.**
   - Publicly, the cover may have worked.
   - Privately, loose conversation can still leak the pattern.
   - This is the quiet turn of the scene.

8. **Exit on attention, not attack.**
   - No chase, no confrontation, no dramatic reveal.
   - The observer remains unidentified.
   - The final feeling: someone is close, listening, and patient.

## Beat Logic

The scene should move from ordinary relief to invisible exposure:

- **Bar normality** creates safety.
- **Earpiece framing** removes that safety.
- **Carlos’s explanation** gives the observer a usable pattern.
- **The observer’s silence** makes the threat feel controlled.
- **The exit** confirms the danger is attention, not immediate violence.

## Guardrails

- Do not reveal the observer’s identity.
- Do not turn the bar into a surveillance center.
- Do not let the crew knowingly brief a room full of strangers.
- Do not overexplain the Bermuda Triangle Fracture Zone.
- Do not add a confrontation or attack.
- Do not make the earpiece tech more important than the listening position.

### EP203_A01S04 — Prying Eyes

**INT. THE SALTY SIREN — NASSAU — NIGHT**

A ceiling fan turns slowly through cigarette haze and sea heat.

The Salty Siren is not trying to be beautiful. Warped wood. Salt-blurred windows. Beer signs with dead bulbs. A radio behind the bar fighting with a tourist laughing too loud near the door.

A server drops two sweating bottles onto a table. Glass knocks wood. Foam crawls down a brown neck.

At the bar, a WOMAN’S HAND rests beside an untouched rum and coke.

That is all we get of her.

No face. No name. No uniform. No mark.

Just the hand. Clean nails. Still fingers. A thumb that does not drum, does not fidget, does not drink.

In her ear, tucked mostly under loose hair, a tiny flesh-colored earpiece catches the room.

The bar noise dulls.

A voice comes through, filtered and close.

**CARLOS (V.O., THROUGH EARPIECE)**  
I’m telling you, it wasn’t the same line. Not the same water, not the same drift, not the same damn sky.

A second voice answers, younger, amused because he wants not to be scared.

**FINNIAN (V.O., THROUGH EARPIECE)**  
The sky was still above us, Carlos. That’s where it usually keeps itself.

A few laughs crackle through the feed.

The woman’s hand slides half an inch toward the glass. Stops.

Across the room, tucked into a corner booth under a mounted marlin with one cloudy eye, CARLOS, MARTIN, FINNIAN, CALLUM, TOBY, and BRAM sit around a table crowded with beers, baskets of fries, wet napkins, and the messy relief of people who have not yet admitted they were afraid.

They are not briefing.

They are coming down.

Bram has both elbows on the table and a bottle held against his forehead. Toby peels the label off his beer in one long nervous strip. Callum keeps glancing toward the front windows by habit. Martin looks like he has been trying to relax for ten minutes and failing.

Carlos has the table because Carlos cannot let the pattern go.

**CARLOS**  
Laugh all you want. The compass didn’t wander. It pulled.

**MARTIN**  
Compasses don’t pull.

**CARLOS**  
This one did.

**TOBY**  
Maybe it just fancied you.

**FINNIAN**  
That would explain the spinning.

Another little burst of laughter. Too loud for one second, then it dies because they all remember the deck under their feet.

At the bar, the woman’s hand remains still.

The earpiece catches everything poorly and well at once: ice in glasses, a chair scrape, Martin’s low voice, Carlos leaning in.

**CARLOS**  
Listen to me. We’ve been near the Triangle before. Skirted it. Watched the instruments get stupid. Watched Gus pretend they weren’t getting stupid.

**CALLUM**  
Gus hears you say that, he’ll make you clean the bilge with your own shirt.

**CARLOS**  
Gus knows exactly what I’m saying. That’s why he turned us off it.

That lands differently.

The table settles.

**BRAM**  
We weren’t going in.

**CARLOS**  
No. We weren’t. That’s the point.

He takes a drink. Not dramatic. Just buying himself one second.

**CARLOS**  
We weren’t trying to go in, and we still got closer than we’ve ever managed.

At the bar, the woman’s thumb presses once against the side of the glass.

**MARTIN**  
Closer to what?

Carlos gives him a look.

Martin knows. They all know. He asked anyway because the word is worse when said out loud.

**CARLOS**  
The Fracture Zone.

The radio behind the bar hits static for half a breath, then finds the song again.

Nobody at the booth notices.

The woman at the bar does.

**FINNIAN**  
You make it sound like a dock.

**CARLOS**  
I wish it was a dock. A dock has posts. Numbers. Rotting rope. Something you can measure without wanting to throw the chart overboard.

**TOBY**  
So what did we measure?

Carlos pulls a damp napkin from under a basket and smooths it on the table. He does not draw a map. He knows better than that in public.

He uses bottle caps instead.

One cap for the Gull. One for Nassau. One for nothing at all.

**CARLOS**  
Here’s where we should’ve been.

He taps the first cap.

**CARLOS**  
Here’s where the radar thought we were.

Second cap.

**CARLOS**  
Here’s where the light broke.

The third cap.

**BRAM**  
Light doesn’t break.

**CALLUM**  
It did.

Callum says it quietly enough that the joke dies before it can be born.

Carlos nods, grateful for the backup.

**CARLOS**  
Two frames. That’s all Linda got. Two frames where the sky gave us aircraft that weren’t there before and weren’t there after.

**MARTIN**  
You’re talking about the five planes.

**FINNIAN**  
The ghost planes.

**CARLOS**  
I’m talking about five aircraft moving through broken light, because if I say ghost planes in a bar in Nassau, I deserve whatever happens next.

A couple of them laugh again, smaller this time.

The woman’s hand moves from the glass to a folded cocktail napkin beside it.

Not writing yet.

Listening.

**TOBY**  
But we’re back. That matters, doesn’t it?

**BRAM**  
Back with passengers thinking they got a very authentic pirate cruise and a free near-death experience.

**FINNIAN**  
They tipped badly for people who survived the impossible.

**MARTIN**  
They don’t know they survived the impossible.

**CALLUM**  
That was the work.

That line has weight because it is true.

The cover worked. The passengers disembarked. The Gull came in under daylight and noise and tourist complaints, not panic.

For one beat, the table lets itself have that.

**BRAM**  
To the work, then.

He lifts his beer.

The others do too, some willing, some tired, some only because not lifting it would mean saying what they are really thinking.

Bottles touch.

**ALL / OVERLAPPING**  
To the work. / Aye. / To not sinking. / To Gus not murdering us.

They drink.

At the bar, the woman does not.

**MARTIN**  
Carlos.

Carlos looks over.

**MARTIN**  
If we got closer without trying, what happens when somebody tries?

The table quiets again.

That is the question nobody wanted to bring to beer.

Carlos does not have a good answer. That bothers him more than the question.

**CARLOS**  
Then they better know what they’re following.

**FINNIAN**  
And if they don’t?

Carlos looks down at the bottle caps.

The cap standing in for nothing-at-all gleams in a puddle of beer.

**CARLOS**  
Then the water teaches them.

A bar laugh erupts from another table. Someone drops a dart. A woman near the jukebox swears at a man who absolutely deserves it.

The Salty Siren rolls on, warm and indifferent.

At the bar, the observer finally moves.

She takes the cocktail napkin.

With a short pencil no one saw her holding, she writes only four words.

**CLOSER THAN BEFORE.**

She pauses.

Then adds one more.

**GULL.**

She folds the napkin once. Twice.

The earpiece catches Carlos again, softer now, half-buried under the return of ordinary conversation.

**CARLOS (V.O., THROUGH EARPIECE)**  
Anyway. Drink your beer. We earned one quiet hour before the world gets clever again.

The woman’s hand closes around the folded napkin.

She leaves the rum and coke untouched.

A few bills appear under the glass.

No rush. No chase. No glance back.

Only the empty stool, the wet ring on the bar, and the crew still laughing in the corner because for one hour they believed the danger had stayed outside.

The front door opens.

Nassau night breathes in.

The observer exits without a face.

The door shuts softly behind her.

**CUT TO BLACK.**
