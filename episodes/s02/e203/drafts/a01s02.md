### EP203_A01S02 — The Gull Plays Dead

EXT. BLOODY GULL — MAIN DECK — DAY

Warm noise rolls up from below deck.

Fiddle music. Applause. Staged laughter. A performer’s pirate growl over a cheap speaker, selling danger to people holding plastic cups and phones.

Then LINDA HAN steps into colder open air.

Her camera is already up.

She is not scared yet. Not fully. Her face has the sharp, terrible focus of someone who has spent her life waiting for the impossible to stop hiding behind rumor and finally step into frame.

The Bloody Gull moves through clean Atlantic water under a sky too bright to be trusted.

Tourists cluster farther aft, laughing at a deckhand in a tricorn hat who pretends to threaten a teenager with a rubber cutlass.

The performance keeps going.

The ship does not.

Not in the old way.

A thin pulse of blue-white light crawls under a seam in the deck near the rail.

Linda catches it.

Her lens tilts down.

The light vanishes.

LINDA
(low, to herself)
No. No, I saw you.

She adjusts focus.

GUS MADDOX stands near the working edge of the deck, half in shadow beneath a coil of rope and rigging. He is not playing pirate. He is not selling the show.

He is watching everything.

Passengers.

Crew flow.

The seams in the deck.

The horizon.

That makes it worse.

Linda lowers the camera just enough to see his face without the frame between them.

LINDA
You saw it.

GUS
Yep.

LINDA
That was under the planks.

GUS
Yep.

LINDA
You got a better word than “yep”?

Gus does not look at her.

GUS
Not one I want the tourists hearing.

Another ripple of staged laughter rises from below. A bell rings for effect. Somewhere, a child yells, delighted.

Linda turns her camera toward the deck seam again.

LINDA
How long?

Gus finally looks at her.

GUS
How long what?

LINDA
How long has she been doing that?

Gus’s silence answers too much.

Before Linda can press him, the hatch to below deck bangs open.

CARLOS VEGA comes up fast with a camera body, a compact playback rig, and a cable looped around his wrist like it tried to escape him.

He looks pale and lit from the inside.

The look of someone who knows he caught something.

And does not yet know what it costs.

CARLOS
Okay. Before anyone yells, I need you to understand the file is intact, but the image is not behaving like an image.

Gus turns.

He does not ask if Carlos saw anything.

GUS
Tell me you got it.

Carlos stops two steps short of them.

CARLOS
I got something.

LINDA
That is not the same sentence.

CARLOS
No, Linda, it is absolutely not.

Linda moves in close, camera still running.

LINDA
Raw file?

CARLOS
Still on card. I have not touched the original. Playback clone only.

LINDA
Good.

GUS
Show me.

Carlos looks past them at the tourists.

The deckhand in the tricorn hat takes a pratfall. The crowd cheers.

Carlos lowers his voice.

CARLOS
Not here.

A soft blue pulse slips under another deck seam.

This time Gus sees it full.

So does Linda.

So does Carlos.

For half a second, none of them move.

Above the stage laughter, the Bloody Gull gives a small wooden groan.

Not loud.

Not theatrical.

Almost pained.

Gus steps closer to Carlos.

GUS
How many passengers have phones out?

CARLOS
All of them. It’s a pirate cruise.

LINDA
Gus.

GUS
How many crew are in costume and not where they’re supposed to be?

CARLOS
Four. Maybe five.

LINDA
Gus.

Gus looks at her now.

LINDA
What did we get close to?

That lands.

Gus scans the horizon.

The water looks ordinary.

That makes it worse.

GUS
Six months.

Linda waits.

Carlos already knows some of it, but even he goes still.

GUS
Six months we’ve been running this stupid floating dinner show as cover. Out and back. Out and back. Different routes. Different times. No pattern a harbor master can hang us with.

A tourist nearby raises a phone for a selfie with the rigging behind her.

Gus lowers his voice further.

GUS
We’ve been probing the Bermuda Triangle Fracture Zone from the real side.

Linda’s camera stays trained on him.

LINDA
You’ve been using civilians as camouflage.

GUS
I’ve been using ticket sales, bad music, and insurance paperwork as camouflage. Civilians are the reason I don’t push closer.

Carlos swallows.

CARLOS
Except today.

Gus turns back to the horizon.

GUS
Today is the closest she’s been in six months.

The ship creaks again, deeper this time.

A pulse flickers beneath the deck seam at Linda’s feet.

Linda frames it, fast.

LINDA
Then this didn’t happen at random.

GUS
No.

Carlos grips the playback rig tighter.

CARLOS
You need to see the capture.

Gus points toward the forward passage.

GUS
Navigation room. Now. Walk normal.

Carlos starts moving.

Linda follows, still filming.

Gus catches her arm before she passes.

Not hard.

Enough.

GUS
If this turns, you stop filming and help me move people.

Linda looks at his hand on her arm.

Then at his face.

LINDA
If this turns, the footage may be the only reason anyone believes what happened to them.

GUS
If this turns wrong, believing us comes second to breathing.

Linda holds his stare.

Then she pulls free.

LINDA
Then buy me both.

They move.

INT. BLOODY GULL — NAVIGATION ROOM — MOMENTS LATER

The navigation room is old brass forced into a truce with modern electronics.

Paper charts are pinned under cracked glass. Taped labels mark patched monitors. Manual override levers sit beside USB hubs, power bricks, solder scars, and a coffee mug full of dead pens.

A radar screen ticks in soft green sweeps.

A second monitor shows exterior deck feeds.

A third monitor carries internal feeds from deeper in the Gull: corridor, hold, machinery shadow, and one low-angle feed pointed into the belly of the ship near the keel.

The Ley Core sits there in the dark.

Not fully seen.

Felt.

A pulse of light moves through the lower ribs of the ship like something breathing in its sleep.

On the chart table, the Spyglass of Storms rests in its cradle.

The Echo of the Tides.

Still.

Too still.

Linda clocks it.

Her camera drifts to it for a beat and holds.

Brass. Glass. Salt-worn edges. A quiet instrument waiting for the one hand not in the room.

No glow.

No answer.

Carlos drops into the transfer station chair and starts connecting the rig.

CARLOS
Playback clone on isolated power. No network. No ship system handshake. Everybody happy?

LINDA
I’ll be happy when the raw card is in a Faraday bag and three clones exist in three different pockets.

CARLOS
That is a very specific version of happiness.

LINDA
I’m a very specific person.

GUS
Show me the damn thing.

Carlos brings up the footage.

On the monitor: a reflection caught in polished brass near the deck stairs. Bad angle. Moving light. Passengers blurring past.

Carlos scrubs back.

Frame by frame.

CARLOS
Okay. Source is not direct lens capture. It’s a reflection off the bell housing near the lower stairwell. Which means normally I would call it useless and make fun of it.

LINDA
Professionally.

CARLOS
With passion.

He advances one frame.

Broken light crawls over the reflection.

Another frame.

The image fractures.

Another.

Five small aircraft appear in formation.

Not models.

Not a graphic.

Not a tourist reflection.

Five aircraft, moving through impossible light.

Then gone.

The room goes very quiet.

Linda leans closer.

LINDA
Back one.

Carlos goes back.

The aircraft return.

Two frames of history where history should not fit.

Gus stares at the monitor.

GUS
Jesus.

Carlos exhales, half laugh, half panic.

CARLOS
Two frames. Forty-eight milliseconds if the timebase is behaving, which it is pretending to do. Reflection source is stable. Timestamp integrity looks clean. Artifact behavior is not compression, not rolling shutter, not a dropped frame, not lens flare unless lens flare has recently developed a World War Two aviation hobby.

LINDA
Five aircraft.

CARLOS
Yes.

LINDA
Flight 19.

No one says anything.

The radar sweep ticks.

Linda’s voice drops. Field-commander tight.

LINDA
Isolate the raw now. Clone it twice. Checksum every copy. Then physically separate the card from anything wired into this ship.

Carlos is already moving.

CARLOS
I need the deck feed, camera timestamp, and internal system logs synced first.

LINDA
No, you need to preserve the negative before the darkroom catches fire.

CARLOS
There is no negative.

LINDA
Carlos.

CARLOS
I know. I know what you mean. But if the ship generated a timestamp echo and we don’t sync it now, we lose causality.

GUS
I need passenger recordings checked.

Linda and Carlos both look at him.

GUS
Now.

LINDA
We have Flight 19 on two frames of live capture.

GUS
And I have one hundred and twelve tourists upstairs who think ghost stories are cute until the deck starts glowing under their sandals.

CARLOS
One hundred and fourteen.

Gus looks at him.

CARLOS
Two comps. Honeymoon couple. They tipped the mermaid.

GUS
Fine. One hundred and fourteen reasons this does not become a public supernatural incident.

LINDA
If passengers caught the glow, deleting or blocking them makes us the cover-up.

GUS
I said checked, not erased.

LINDA
Good.

GUS
Outgoing signals contained.

Linda bristles.

GUS
Contained, Linda. Not destroyed. If this thing starts reaching through electronics, I don’t want some livestream opening a door in a dentist’s office in Tampa.

Carlos looks up from the transfer station.

CARLOS
That is a very specific fear.

GUS
I’ve had six months to get specific.

A low pulse rolls through the navigation room lights.

The modern monitors flicker.

The brass compass mounted near the chart table trembles once.

The Spyglass of Storms remains still in its cradle.

Linda’s camera catches it again.

Longer this time.

The lens finds the inert glass, the salt in the seams, the dead-black reflection where a living answer ought to be.

Then she pulls back to Carlos’s monitor.

GUS
Carlos.

CARLOS
Yeah.

GUS
Find me why now.

Carlos switches the lower internal feed to full screen.

The Ley Core image fills the monitor.

Dark keel-space. Old wood. Ley-etched geometry pulsing under layers of shadow and repair.

Carlos scrubs the feed backward.

The timestamp rolls.

CARLOS
Backing from current internal. Keel feed has minor distortion, but the clock is still readable.

Linda shifts closer, camera over his shoulder.

LINDA
Match the aircraft capture.

CARLOS
That is what I am doing.

LINDA
Do it faster.

CARLOS
That is also what I am doing.

He stops.

The Ley Core pulses hard.

A clean flare of blue-white light climbs through the ship’s lower ribs.

Carlos brings the aircraft capture into a second window.

Same timestamp.

Linda’s deck footage in a third window.

The seam at her feet pulses.

Same timestamp.

Carlos overlays the ship’s route data.

Same timestamp.

His excitement drains into something colder.

CARLOS
There.

LINDA
Say it clean.

CARLOS
Aircraft bleed, deck seam glow, Ley Core surge. Same moment. Same timestamp.

GUS
Triggered by proximity.

CARLOS
That is the least insane explanation, yes.

LINDA
The fracture reached for the ship.

Gus watches the Ley Core pulse again, softer now.

GUS
Or she reached back.

The Bloody Gull creaks.

This time the sound moves through the room like an old animal shifting in pain.

The walls answer with a faint timber shiver.

Above them, muffled laughter continues. Music. A cheer from the fake pirate show.

Down here, no one breathes for a second.

It does not feel like an attack.

It feels like recognition.

Or grief.

Linda lowers the camera a fraction.

LINDA
That boy said it felt sad.

Gus looks at her.

LINDA
Earlier. On deck. When the light hit.

Carlos glances between them.

CARLOS
Children are unfortunately good at noticing the thing everyone else is pretending not to notice.

The radar emits a soft warning chirp.

Once.

Gus turns.

On the navigation monitor, the plotted route line sits safely outside the Bermuda Triangle boundary Carlos marked weeks ago in red.

Then the screen glitches.

The map jumps.

For one clean second, the Bloody Gull’s position relocates inside the triangle.

Deep inside.

The radar sweep bends around a shape that is not there.

Then the monitor snaps back.

Ordinary Atlantic.

Ordinary route.

Ordinary lie.

Gus steps closer to the screen.

GUS
Did you do that?

Carlos’s hands lift away from the keyboard.

CARLOS
I did not do that.

Linda has the camera back up.

LINDA
Route says we’re outside.

CARLOS
We are outside.

LINDA
Map says we weren’t.

CARLOS
For one second.

LINDA
One second is enough.

Gus stares at the radar.

Another sweep.

Nothing.

Another.

Still nothing.

The ship gives a smaller creak beneath them. Almost apologetic.

Gus makes the decision.

GUS
We turn around.

Linda turns on him.

LINDA
Gus.

GUS
Now.

CARLOS
If we turn, we lose the approach vector.

GUS
Good.

LINDA
This is the closest you’ve gotten in six months.

GUS
And the first time she lit up with civilians aboard.

LINDA
We have proof.

GUS
You have proof if we get it home.

That stops her.

Gus keys the old brass intercom and the modern radio at the same time.

GUS
All hands, shift to return pattern. Quiet feet. No alarm. I want deck lights up, music high, and every smiling bastard we have in costume visible in two minutes.

A beat.

Then crew acknowledgments crackle back.

CREW VOICES
(over radio)
Copy.

On it.

Return pattern.

Gus keeps going.

GUS
Tell Bram I want the lantern line lit stem to stern. String lights, show lights, cheap skull lights, I don’t care. If the deck glows again, I want the passengers thinking we paid extra for it.

CARLOS
That is either brilliant or deeply depressing.

GUS
Both is fine.

Linda watches him.

LINDA
You’re not burying this.

Gus turns from the radio.

GUS
No.

He points to Carlos.

GUS
Save everything fast.

Then to Linda.

GUS
Film what you can without starting a stampede.

Linda holds his stare.

GUS
I’m buying minutes. That’s all.

Above them, the music changes.

Louder. Brighter. Ridiculous.

A chorus of tourist cheers rises as the deck lights begin clicking on one section at a time.

On the exterior feed, warm string lights bloom across the railings. Decorative lanterns flare gold. Fake pirate spectacle spreads over the ship like a painted smile.

Under Carlos’s hands, transfer windows multiply.

RAW_CAPTURE_CLONE_A — 12%

SYSTEM_LOG_SYNC — 8%

DECK_FEED_EXPORT — 15%

Carlos pulls a small shielded pouch from a drawer with shaking fingers.

CARLOS
Raw card comes out after Clone A verifies.

LINDA
Checksum?

CARLOS
Running.

LINDA
Second clone?

CARLOS
Queued.

LINDA
Physical separation?

CARLOS
Linda, if you ask me one more preservation question, I am going to become religious in a very inconvenient way.

LINDA
Good. Fear improves workflow.

Gus takes the wheel control lever mounted near the navigation station and eases it over.

Somewhere deep in the ship, old mechanisms answer modern command.

The Bloody Gull begins to turn.

Slowly.

Reluctantly.

The room tilts with the motion.

On the map, the plotted route curves back toward ordinary water, ordinary docks, ordinary lies that keep people alive.

The Ley Core pulse on the internal feed settles.

Almost.

Linda films the monitors.

Carlos watches the transfer crawl.

Gus watches the radar.

For a moment, the map behaves.

The Gull’s position returns to open Atlantic water.

Clean.

Safe.

False, maybe, but safe-looking.

Then the radar sweep ticks again.

At the far edge of the screen, five tiny ghosts flicker where no aircraft should be.

A formation.

There for less than a breath.

Gone.

Carlos sees it.

Linda sees Carlos see it.

Gus sees both of them.

No one says Flight 19 this time.

Above them, the deck erupts in cheerful tourist applause.

Below them, the transfer bar crawls forward.

The Bloody Gull keeps turning.

FADE OUT.

Jesus.

Five aircraft.

Yes.

Flight 19.

Carlos.

I need passenger recordings checked.

Now.

We have Flight 19 on two frames of live capture.

One hundred and fourteen.

Outgoing signals contained.

I’ve had six months to get specific.

Carlos.
