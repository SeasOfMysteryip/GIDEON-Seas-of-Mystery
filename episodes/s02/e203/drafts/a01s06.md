## EP203_A01S06 — The Water Answers Wrong

### EXT. NASSAU DOCK — DAY

The Bloody Gull sits too still against a modern Nassau dock.

Around her: charter boats, fuel carts, tourists with shopping bags, a dockhand dragging a hose, somebody laughing too loud into a phone. Ordinary noise. Ordinary sun. Ordinary water.

The Gull almost passes.

Almost.

GUS works the deck with clipped focus, checking lines, sightlines, passengers, dock traffic. He has a radio in one hand and a coil of line under one boot.

LINDA stands near the gangway with her camera rig low against her hip, one eye on the dock, one eye on a small monitor. The image flickers with compression scars.

JEB is not looking at the dock.

He is looking at the water.

LARK stands beside a crate of gear with the Spyglass wrapped in cloth under her arm. She is trying to listen to Gus and failing.

GUS
We are out of time. Not low on time. Out.

LINDA
You said that six minutes ago.

GUS
Yeah, and then six minutes got murdered.

He points toward the dock entrance without making it obvious.

GUS
Two phones on us. One dock camera that keeps pretending it ain’t pointed this way. Harbor guy has walked past us three times without needing to walk past us once.

LINDA
And I’ve got missing frames from the same six-minute window. Which means something happened in the window.

GUS
Something always happens in the window. That’s why people get shot standing in one.

Linda doesn’t look up.

LINDA
This isn’t optics, Gus.

GUS
Everything is optics until it gets us boarded.

Jeb’s eyes stay on the waterline.

JEB
Boarded ain’t what worries me.

That lands. Quietly.

Gus glances at him.

GUS
Captain, with love and deep professional irritation, I need a go or no-go.

Jeb does not answer fast enough.

Lark shifts. The Spyglass knocks lightly against the crate.

A small sound.

Too small to stop anyone.

It stops her.

She looks down at the wrapped relic.

LINDA
Lark?

Lark does not answer.

Gus steps toward the bowline.

GUS
Cast off forward. We idle clean, we smile at the nice people, and we do not give Nassau a second miracle before lunch.

A crewman reaches for the cleat.

LARK
Don’t.

The word cuts across the dock.

The crewman freezes.

Gus turns slowly.

GUS
That better be attached to a reason.

Lark stares at the water beside the Gull.

Nothing visible. Just sun. Ripple. Hull-shadow.

LARK
It’s wrong.

GUS
The whole week is wrong. Pick a lane.

LARK
The water.

Linda raises the camera a fraction.

LINDA
What about it?

Lark unwraps the Spyglass without looking away from the surface.

LARK
It’s answering before we ask.

Gus exhales through his nose. Not dismissive. Not quite. But every second hurts him.

GUS
No. No riddles. Not right now.

LARK
I’m not giving you a riddle.

GUS
Then give me a thing I can act on.

Lark brings the Spyglass up.

For a moment, she sees only Nassau through the old glass: dock planks, white hulls, hard noon light.

Then the image bends.

Not a vision blooming open. Not a clean supernatural reveal.

A misanswer.

The reflection below the Gull shows pilings that are not here. Darker wood. Older rope. A narrow harbor mouth under a dead-gray sky.

Saltcrow Haven, pressed thin beneath Nassau.

Lark lowers the Spyglass fast, as if it burned her.

The water looks normal.

She raises it again.

The wrong harbor is still there.

Only in the reflection.

Only around the Gull.

LARK
We can’t leave.

GUS
We absolutely can.

LARK
Not through that.

GUS
Through water?

LARK
Through a door pretending to be water.

Jeb’s expression changes almost not at all.

But his hand closes on the rail.

Linda clocks it.

LINDA
Jeb?

JEB
Let her look.

Gus hates that answer.

GUS
Captain—

JEB
Let her look.

The dock noise thins.

Not silence. Worse. The wrong sounds remain underneath the right ones. A gull cry that doesn’t match the sky. A rope creak from a ship that isn’t there. A low slap of water against stone instead of fiberglass and treated dock posts.

Linda lifts her camera fully now.

LINDA
I’m rolling.

GUS
Of course you are.

LINDA
You want proof or comfort?

GUS
I want mobility.

LINDA
Then you’re gonna hate proof.

Linda steps closer to the edge and angles the lens down.

On her monitor: the water around the Gull tears into blocky digital noise. The dock planks remain clean. The neighboring boat remains clean. The Gull’s reflection does not.

The reflection shows a harbor that is not Nassau.

Then the image snaps back.

Linda checks the timestamp.

LINDA
That’s not camera shake.

Gus moves to her shoulder.

GUS
Could be heat distortion.

LINDA
On one reflection? Around one hull? With the timestamp dropping two frames and the water moving the wrong direction?

Gus looks at the actual water.

A faint current pulls along the dock, visible in floating foam and a loose strip of paper.

The mooring line tugs against it.

The wrong way.

Gus’s face goes still.

That is the first thing he can act on.

GUS
Everybody off the lines.

The crewman steps back.

Lark keeps staring through the Spyglass.

LARK
It’s closer when we move.

GUS
You don’t know that.

LARK
The Gull knows it.

The hull gives a low wooden complaint.

Not loud. Not theatrical.

Enough.

Jeb looks down like an old friend has flinched.

A thin shimmer crawls along the waterline. It is not bright enough for the whole dock to notice. Not lightning. Not magic for tourists.

Just a wrong sheen in the ordinary sun.

Nassau’s blue water darkens under the Gull.

For half a breath, the dock reflection no longer matches the dock.

The gangway reflected in the water leads somewhere narrower. Older. Wet stone instead of sun-bleached boards. A hanging lantern sways where no lantern hangs.

Linda whispers without meaning to.

LINDA
Saltcrow.

Lark lowers the Spyglass.

Her face is pale, furious, vindicated, and afraid.

LARK
It’s not showing us a place.

Jeb finally looks at her.

LARK
It’s trying to decide which place this is.

Gus looks from Lark to Jeb.

GUS
Tell me she’s wrong.

Jeb watches the water answer Nassau, then not Nassau, then Nassau again.

JEB
Wish I could.

That is worse than an explanation.

Another tug hits the lines.

The Gull shifts against the fenders, but the surrounding water pulls inward, toward her hull, against the open harbor.

A dockhand fifty yards away glances over, confused by the noise. He sees nothing worth understanding and keeps walking.

The event is local.

Private enough to be survivable.

Specific enough to be damning.

Linda checks her monitor again.

LINDA
I’ve got corruption on the visual, timestamp drift, and water motion contradicting the tide. If anybody else is watching this—

GUS
They are.

No joke in it.

Linda looks at him.

GUS
Assume they are.

Lark tightens the cloth around the Spyglass, but the relic hums under her fingers.

LARK
If we cast off now, we don’t know what we’re casting into.

Gus looks toward open water. The exit lane is clear. It should be the safest direction in the world.

It is not.

He keys his radio.

GUS
Hold departure. Secure all lines. Nobody steps off. Nobody steps on. We are not moving until I say we’re moving.

A beat.

GUS
And somebody kill the smile for the tourists. We’re a haunted pirate cruise, not a hostage situation.

No one laughs.

Gus clips the radio back to his belt and looks at Lark.

GUS
You just cost us our window.

Lark meets him.

LARK
I know.

GUS
You better be right.

Jeb’s eyes stay on the water.

JEB
She was early.

The water goes normal all at once.

No glow. No wrong harbor. No counter-current.

Just Nassau again.

That is not a comfort.

Linda lowers the camera.

LINDA
It stopped.

JEB
No.

He looks out past the dock, past the boats, past the ordinary horizon.

JEB
It learned where we are.

### INT. UNMARKED OBSERVATION ROOM — DAY

A dark monitor wall. Clean desks. No decoration.

A live harbor feed sits beside a pale map-grid and a narrow instrument readout pulsing in measured lines.

LT. ALINA VOSS watches without leaning forward.

She does not look excited.

She looks corrected.

On one screen, the Bloody Gull is just another vessel profile among too many hulls and masts.

On another, a thin spike rises and collapses.

Then rises again.

A TECH, off to the side, checks the readout.

TECH
That’s the same signature?

Voss keeps her eyes on the vessel image.

VOSS
Same shape. Different water.

The spike flickers once more.

The harbor camera glitches for two frames.

Voss marks the location.

VOSS
Keep eyes on Nassau.

TECH
Intercept?

Voss watches the Gull sit motionless at the dock.

VOSS
Not yet.

A beat.

VOSS
First we find out why it stopped running.

On the monitor, the water around the Bloody Gull looks perfectly ordinary.

The reader disagrees.

## Recommended Scene Spine — Docks Version With Lark
## EP203_A01S06 — The Water Answers Wrong

### Scene Job
End the act by proving the crew's concealment model has failed. They are not only being watched; the world around the Bloody Gull is beginning to behave like the Spectral Sea.

### Entry State
The crew believes their immediate danger is exposure: modern systems, witnesses, records, civilians, cameras, radar, and official attention.

### Exit State
The sea around the Gull behaves impossibly in ordinary waters. The crew understands the breach is active. The unseen watcher is revealed as Lt. Alina Voss, who now has enough to know something impossible has resurfaced.

### Beat Spine
1. **Dockside prep under pressure.** Jeb, Gus, and Linda work around the Bloody Gull, getting ready to move while trying not to draw attention.
2. **The practical argument.** Gus frames the immediate risk as operational: people, systems, visibility, and movement. Linda presses that the evidence trail is already too strange to vanish cleanly. Jeb reads the sea and does not like how quiet it is.
3. **Lark’s active warning.** If included, Lark has the Spyglass/Echo nearby or in hand. It reacts wrong — not a full vision dump, just enough to show the instrument recognizes something before the others do.
4. **The water answers wrong.** Around the Gull, ordinary dock water shows Spectral behavior: glow where there should be none, current moving against itself, or a brief visual overlap between Nassau and Saltcrow Haven.
5. **Recognition, not explanation.** The crew does not solve it. They recognize it. Jeb knows this is not normal sea behavior. Linda knows this cannot be treated as a camera artifact. Gus knows this changes the risk model immediately.
6. **The turn.** Concealment is no longer enough. The problem is not just who sees them; it is that reality is beginning to leak around the Gull.
7. **Act-out reveal.** Cut away from the docks. The watcher thread resolves: Lt. Alina Voss receives, reviews, or cross-matches the anomaly trail. She is not guessing anymore.

### Clean End Button
Voss should not say too much. The button works best if her reaction is controlled, trained, and specific: she has found a pattern, not a ghost story. She now knows something impossible has resurfaced.

## Lark Inclusion Test
## Use Lark If...

- The Spyglass/Echo does something active and scene-relevant.
- Lark detects the wrong harbor pattern before the others fully understand it.
- Her presence increases urgency instead of adding explanation.
- She changes behavior: warns them, stops them, confirms the bleed, or forces the crew to look where they would not have looked.

## Cut Lark If...

- She only stands nearby while Jeb, Gus, and Linda discuss the plan.
- The Spyglass/Echo becomes decorative.
- Her dialogue repeats what the water already shows.

## Best Use
Lark should be the first alarm, not the scene lecturer. The water becomes the proof.

## Spectral Bleed Rule For This Scene
## Working Rule
The breach is localized to the Bloody Gull's immediate presence at the docks. The ordinary sea does not fully become the Spectral Sea. It briefly answers with the wrong harbor.

- Current moving the wrong way around the Gull.
- A momentary overlay of Nassau/Saltcrow Haven against the modern dock water.

- The Spyglass/Echo catching the wrong harbor image before the naked eye does.

## What It Should Not Do Yet
- Open a stable portal.
- Move the crew physically to Saltcrow Haven.
- Cause Voss to arrive in person.
- Explain the entire breach mechanism.
- Establish a permanent new travel rule.

## Voss End Button — No Meetup Version
## Function
Reveal that the watcher is Lt. Alina Voss and that she now has enough pattern evidence to stop treating the anomaly as noise.

## Shape
The final movement should be separate from the docks. Voss is reviewing or cross-matching fragments: water behavior, positional irregularity, impossible light, timing, or anomaly trail. She does not need to understand the Spectral Sea. She only needs to know the impossible event is real and repeatable enough to pursue.

## Button Logic
- She is trained.
- She is not credulous.
- She is not guessing anymore.
- She does not meet the crew yet.

## Best Final Feeling
Not villain entrance. Not exposition dump. A competent observer quietly realizing the world has opened a door it should not have.
