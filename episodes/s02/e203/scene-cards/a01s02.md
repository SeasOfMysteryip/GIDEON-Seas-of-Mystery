## Scene Purpose

Prove the Bermuda Triangle Fracture Zone is reacting to the Bloody Gull's proximity and force Gus to choose passenger safety over deeper investigation.

## Entry State

Carlos has a strange capture from A1S1. Linda and Gus have witnessed deck-seam glow. The pirate-cruise cover is still holding below decks.

## Turn

Carlos syncs the Flight 19 image, Ley Core surge, and deck glow to the same timestamp. The ship is not merely haunted; the fracture is bleeding into real-world position.

## Exit State

Gus orders the Gull turned away. Linda and Carlos preserve the evidence. The crew masks the supernatural glow with cruise lights. The fracture remains active enough to promise worse.

## Emotional Owner

Gus. He is not the lore man; he is the officer turning terror into procedure.

## Scene Pulse

- The Bloody Gull is the closest she has been to the Bermuda Triangle Fracture Zone in six months of probing under pirate-cruise cover.
- Carlos and Linda keep their documentary-production energy: footage, timestamps, frame count, signal integrity, and proof.
- Gus operates as ship-first command pressure: passengers, crew, containment, turn the ship.
- The Flight 19 image, deck-seam glow, and Ley Core surge are one proximity event, not separate spectacles.
- The ship behaves like a character through distress, recognition, or grief, but the scene does not turn that into a magical conversation.
- The Spyglass of Storms / Echo of the Tides sits inactive in its cradle as Lark absence pressure.

## Repaired Beat Spine

1. Linda reaches the deck first. She comes up out of the warm noise of the pirate cruise into colder open air, camera already up. Gus hangs back near the working edge of the deck, watching passengers, crew movement, deck seams, and horizon.

2. Carlos arrives with the capture. He has the look of someone who knows he caught something but does not know what it costs. Gus says, "Tell me you got it." Linda saw the light pulsing below the deck seams. So did Gus.

3. Gus names the operational context. They have been running the pirate cruise for six months as cover while probing the Bermuda Triangle Fracture Zone from the real world. This is the closest the Bloody Gull has been in that window.

4. Carlos shows the two-frame aircraft image. Five small aircraft appear for two frames, moving through broken light. Carlos reads it in production language: frame count, reflection source, artifact behavior, timestamp integrity. Linda clocks the evidentiary problem: two frames is enough to matter and not enough to explain.

5. Linda pushes preservation; Gus pushes ship safety. Linda wants the raw footage cloned, checksumed, and physically isolated. Carlos wants the camera, deck feed, and system logs synced. Gus wants passenger recordings checked, outgoing signals contained, and crew moved without alarming tourists.

6. They move into the navigation room. The room is a hybrid bunker: old brass, patched modern monitors, taped labels, manual overrides, paper charts, and Carlos's transfer station. One monitor shows exterior navigation/radar/map data. Another shows deeper internal feeds, including the Ley Core near the keel. The Spyglass of Storms / Echo of the Tides sits in its cradle on the chart table as a quiet Lark absence marker.

7. Carlos syncs the event instead of starting a concealment protocol. The Ley Core feed timestamp matches the Flight 19 image capture and the deck-seam glow. The answer to why now is proximity to the fracture zone, not a generic cloaking failure.

8. The Gull responds. A low creak moves through the ship. It should feel like distress, recognition, or grief, not attack. The boy's earlier line, "It felt sad," echoes without being explained.

9. The map/radar contradicts reality. Gus checks the radar/map monitor. Their plotted route says they are outside the Bermuda Triangle. The monitor glitches and briefly relocates them inside it. This is the one clean navigation contradiction.

10. Gus orders the cover and the turn. He calls hands to deck and orders the ship turned around. The crew lights the deck with decorative cruise lights so any renewed seam glow reads as part of the tourist act. He tells Linda and Carlos to save everything fast: local, no broadcast, no hero uploads.

11. Exit image. Carlos's transfer bar crawls across the monitor while the ship begins her turn. The deck above blooms with cheerful tourist lights. Under the floor, the Ley Core pulse settles, almost. On the map, the Gull returns to ordinary Atlantic water. A dead channel carries, "Everything looks wrong."

## Working Rule Candidate

When the Bloody Gull operates near ordinary-world systems, the crew can make her pass as a modern tourist vessel only by actively suppressing spectral bleed and over-performing normalcy. The Fracture Zone weakens that cover.

## Discipline Notes

- Remove broad "concealment protocol" language. The better action is operational improvisation: protect passengers, mask the glow, save evidence, turn the ship.
- Do not make this a stable Bloody Gull cloaking mode.
- Keep Flight 19, deck seams, Ley Core surge, and one map/radar displacement. Do not stack compass drift, impossible depth, wake distortion, dead-channel coordinates, and other symptoms.
- Gus should not forbid recording. He orders: save it fast, keep it local, no external transmission until clear.
- Let the Echo/Spyglass sit in the cradle as absence pressure. Do not activate it in this scene.
- Linda should be sharp, not reckless. Carlos should read the impossible through footage, artifacting, timestamp sync, and signal behavior.

## Dialogue Pressure Notes

- Gus: "Six months we've been skirting this line under a pirate-cruise flag. This is the closest she's let us get."
- Linda: "Two frames is not nothing. Two frames is exactly how history tries to disappear."

- Gus: "Save it local. No cloud, no broadcast, no hero uploads. Fast."
- Linda: "I hate leaving the first real answer we've had."
- Gus: "Then hate it while we turn around."
