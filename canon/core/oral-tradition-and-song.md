# Oral Tradition And Song

Songs, chants, and repeated deck-tales are part of how `Seas of Mystery` stores memory inside the world.

## Canon Rule

Song in this setting may function as:

- warning language
- crew tradition
- relic stabilization or tuning behavior
- memory preservation
- faction or ship identity

It should not be treated as random decoration.

## Old Red Rule

Old Red's voice may legitimately draw from:

- warning-song fragments
- broken shanty lines
- marrow-cycle refrains
- rhythmic omen language

That sourcing should feel inherited, not improv-comedy and not generic pirate verse.

## Bloody Gull Rule

The Bloody Gull carries oral culture.

That means crew stories, chants, and repeated lines can:

- preserve prior battles
- encode warnings
- carry morale and rebellion memory
- keep the ship feeling lived-in across time

## The Bloody Gull Boys Rule

The Bloody Gull Boys are the named shipboard act for the Bloody Gull's working music culture. They are not a separate faction and not a relic system. They are crew first: sailors, repair hands, riggers, runners, and morale workers whose songs can become useful when the ship needs public cover or deck rhythm.

Current named members:

- Callum Thorne: topman, guitar, vocals.
- Bram Pike: carpenter's mate, upright bass, low harmony.
- Finnian Bell: sailmaker-rigger, fiddle, accordion, high harmony or response lines.
- Toby Marlowe: bosun's runner, drums, response vocals.

Their music may support morale, oral tradition, timing, concealment, and cover noise. It must not turn them into mystic singers, prophecy channels, or major plot engines unless a separate canon file establishes that rule.

## Boundary Rule

Not every source-lore lyric is hard canon.

What becomes canon-relevant is:

- the function of song
- recurring adopted refrains
- any line or pattern already embedded in scripts or locked canon files
